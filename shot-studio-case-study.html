01
Project Overview

A STUDIO BRAND
BUILT IN A BROWSER

SHOT is a self-initiated design project — a fictional photography studio website conceived and executed as a personal challenge in expressive UI design. The brief was simple: design a website for a photography brand that doesn't just present work, but embodies the energy and attitude of the studio itself through every visual and layout decision.

Photography studio websites occupy a competitive and often predictable category. Most fall into one of two camps: the bare, monochrome portfolio that lets the work speak entirely for itself, or the overdesigned showreel that distracts from it. SHOT was an attempt to find a third path — bold visual personality with a clear, functional structure that respects the user's time while making them feel something the moment the page loads.

Design premise: The website UI should feel like a photograph — every element deliberately framed, every colour choice intentional, every piece of negative space earning its keep. A visitor should understand the studio's creative identity within the first three seconds of landing on the page.

02
The Design Brief

WHAT MAKES A
PHOTOGRAPHER'S
WEBSITE WORK

Before opening Figma, I audited a wide range of photography studio websites — from boutique portrait studios to large commercial agencies — to identify where most of them were failing their own brief. The pattern was consistent: visually impressive but functionally flat, or functionally clear but completely forgettable.

The Audience

The target user for SHOT is someone who has already decided they want a photographer — they're not being sold the concept of photography, they're choosing between studios. That changes the design imperative: the job of the homepage is not to explain what photography is, it's to communicate what makes this studio different and make it effortless to take the next step.

  • Primary users — individuals and families booking portrait, graduation, or event photography
  • Secondary users — brands and businesses looking for commercial or product photography
  • Both audiences need to feel the studio is professional, creative, and approachable — not intimidating or overly corporate
  • Mobile browsing is the primary discovery channel — the design had to hold up at every breakpoint

"THE UI SHOULD FEEL LIKE A DELIBERATE FRAME — EVERY ELEMENT IN ITS PLACE FOR A REASON."

03
Key Design Decisions

EVERY CHOICE
TELLS PART
OF THE STORY

The Logo Mark

The SHOT wordmark replaces the letter "O" with a camera aperture symbol — a simple typographic substitution that communicates the brand's identity without needing an icon. Clever without being clever-for-its-own-sake.

Crimson Red as Primary

Red in photography has a specific cultural weight — darkrooms, camera indicator lights, the urgency of capturing a moment. Using it as the primary brand colour ties the visual identity directly to the medium itself.

Abstract Shape System

The background uses large geometric circles and flowing contour lines — non-representational shapes that create depth and movement without competing with the subject photography that dominates the hero.

Cutout Photography

The subject is placed over, not within, the background circles — using a cutout/removal technique that creates a layered composition and gives the hero a sense of three-dimensional depth on a flat screen.

Headline Typography

The hero headline uses a bold sans-serif in all caps at a large scale — the kind of type treatment that belongs on a billboard or a magazine cover, not a typical web portfolio. Sets the tone immediately.

Outline CTA Treatment

The primary CTA "Get a shot" uses a red outline-only button style rather than a filled button — a deliberate choice that feels artistic and confident rather than generic, while still reading as clearly interactive.

04
Colour & Visual Language

A PALETTE AS
INTENTIONAL AS
THE LENS

Photography studios live or die on their visual taste. The colour system needed to feel curated — like a studio that makes deliberate aesthetic choices — rather than generic or trend-chasing.

Crimson Red
#C41230

Brand primary. Headline accents, CTAs, nav button. Energy, urgency, passion.

Near Black
#0A0A0A

Typography and nav. Not pure black — a barely-there warmth that softens the contrast.

Sage Mist
#B2C9C0

Background circle accent. Cool, calm counterpoint to the warmth of crimson and skin tones.

Near White
#FAFAFA

Page background. Gives the hero a light, airy feel — lets the photography breathe.

The Colour Logic

Three of the four colours are effectively neutral — near-black, near-white, and a muted sage. This restraint is intentional: in a photography portfolio, the images themselves carry the colour. The UI's job is to frame them, not compete with them. The singular use of crimson red ensures every interactive element and brand accent lands with maximum impact precisely because it appears nowhere else.

05
Layout Exploration

TWO TAKES ON
THE SAME HERO

The two screens represent a deliberate layout exploration — testing how the same core content and visual system behaves across two different compositions. Both use identical copy, colour, and imagery. The only variable is the framing of the subject within the hero circle.

Variant A Selected Direction
Variant B Alternate Explore
  • Variant A — The subject is cropped mid-frame by the top edge of the circle, creating a contained, portrait-style composition that feels focused and intimate
  • Variant B — The circle crops lower, revealing more of the subject and giving the hero a wider, more cinematic feel — slightly less personal, more editorial
  • Variant A was selected as the primary direction because the tighter crop creates a stronger connection between the viewer and the subject — more appropriate for a portrait-led studio
  • Both variants demonstrate that the underlying design system is flexible enough to produce meaningfully different moods without changing a single colour or type decision
06
Takeaways

WHAT PERSONAL
PROJECTS TEACH
YOU DIFFERENTLY

Personal projects are where you take the risks a client brief won't always let you take. SHOT was a chance to push typographic scale, play with unconventional layout compositions, and make strong aesthetic choices without a committee to answer to. The lessons that came out of it have influenced how I approach all identity-driven UI work since.

🔤

Typography can be a brand

The wordmark is just text with one substitution. No custom illustration, no complex symbol. The right typographic decision at the right scale IS the identity.

🎨

Restraint amplifies impact

Using crimson in just three places across the entire page made each of those three moments hit harder than if it had appeared everywhere. Colour scarcity creates colour power.

📐

Shape creates depth without imagery

The background circles and contour lines do compositional work — they create foreground, midground, and background on a flat surface without a single additional photograph.

🔁

Explore variants before committing

Building two hero compositions from the same system revealed things about the design that a single version never would. The comparison made the selected direction feel like a real decision, not a default.

🖱️

The CTA is part of the tone

An outline button where a filled button was expected is a micro-personality moment. Small choices like this accumulate into the feeling that a brand has a genuine point of view.

📸

Design for the medium's context

Photography UI should feel like photography — framed, composed, intentional. The best portfolio sites don't just host images, they are themselves images of a designer's sensibility.
