Project Overview & The Client Brief

A Real Kitchen.
A Real Problem to Solve.

Mr. Chukwudi Okorie runs Chuks Kitchen — a Nigerian home-kitchen brand with a loyal customer base and a growing appetite for scale. But the ordering operation was entirely manual: every order came through WhatsApp messages and phone calls. No structured menu, no consistent order flow, no way to scale without things falling apart.

He arrived with a comprehensive Brand Requirement Document and one clear goal — design a mobile app that makes ordering as enjoyable as the food. The experience needed to feel warm, energetic, and distinctly Chuks Kitchen, while remaining effortless to use.

Core brief: Design a production-ready mobile ordering app that replaces manual WhatsApp-based order-taking, strictly adheres to the BRD, and gives Chuks Kitchen a digital presence worthy of its reputation — capturing the brand's vibrant, friendly personality without sacrificing usability.

Design System & Colour Language

A Colour System Built
for Hierarchy

The BRD specified orange, pink, and blue as the three brand pillars — but the real design work was deciding how to deploy each one. The full colour system defined shades from 100 to 800 across every palette, giving the UI precise tonal control rather than forcing harsh all-or-nothing colour use.

How the Palette Was Applied in the UI

Primary Orange#FF6B00 · Default 500
CTA Hover#FC8630 · 400
Secondary Pink#FF4B91 · Default 500
Accent Blue#0056D2 · Default 500
Neutral White#FFFFFF · Base
  • Orange (Primary) — Reserved for all primary CTAs, active nav states, and key interactive elements. Never used decoratively.
  • Pink (Secondary) — Used for the hero promotional banner on the home screen, creating a focal point without competing with orange CTAs.
  • Blue (Accent) — Deployed sparingly for links ("Forgot password?", "Login", "Sign up") — semantic differentiation for interactive text.
  • White + Neutrals — Backgrounds, card surfaces, and form fields. Keeps the UI breathing and prevents colour fatigue.

Every Requirement

A brief tells you what — design has to figure out how. Before touching a single frame, I mapped every functional requirement from the BRD to a specific UI implication. This kept all design decisions grounded in real business goals, not aesthetic preference alone.

04
The UX/UI Challenge

Energetic Brand.
Frictionless Experience.
Both at Once.

The central tension in this project is one that shows up often in food-tech design: the brand wants to be loud; the user wants to order fast. Orange as a dominant CTA colour works beautifully. Orange everywhere becomes noise. Every UI decision had to answer one question: does this help the user order faster, or does it just make the screen feel more exciting?

Brand Energy vs UI Calm

Bold colours needed to feel intentional at every use. Orange had to earn its place on every screen — not appear by default.

BRD Gaps to Navigate

Onboarding flow, error states, and payment UI were unspecified. Reasoned design decisions had to be made and clearly documented.

Mobile-First Hierarchy

Small screens demand ruthless prioritisation. Every screen had to communicate its single purpose within the first two seconds of viewing.

"The best UI for an energetic brand isn't the one that looks most energetic — it's the one that makes users feel confident enough to order without thinking twice."

05
Design Process — Lo-Fi to Hi-Fi

The Work Before
the Work

Every hi-fi screen began as a lo-fi wireframe. The wireframing phase was used to lock in layout logic, information hierarchy, and interaction flow — before any colour, imagery, or brand personality was applied. This approach meant that when the visual layer arrived, it elevated a structure that was already sound.

A

Onboarding & Authentication

First impression, trust-building, and zero-friction entry

The wireframe established a simple top-to-bottom reading flow: image block → headline → supporting text → single CTA. The hi-fi applied a full-bleed food photograph in place of the image placeholder — real Nigerian jollof and grilled fish, doing the selling before a single word is read. The orange "Get started" button carries the brand's primary colour at the only decision point on screen.

The wireframe placed two form fields and two CTAs — Login (primary) and Continue as Guest (secondary outline). The hi-fi respected this hierarchy exactly, using the orange fill for Login and an orange-outline-only style for Guest — reducing account-creation drop-off without demoting the primary path.

The verification screen wireframe had a simple structure: instruction text, one input, one CTA. The hi-fi transformed this waiting state into a warm brand moment — a friendly envelope illustration with cooking icons replaced the cold empty space, and the copy was warmed up to reduce anxiety during what is inherently a pause in the user's journey.

B

Core Ordering Experience

Home, Menu, and Product Detail — where conversion lives

The home wireframe laid out a clear scan-path: logo + notification → search → promo banner → category pills → meal grid → bottom nav. The hi-fi kept this structure intact and used the pink promotional banner — the secondary brand colour's only major appearance in the UI — to create a focal point that draws the eye without competing with the orange navigation system.

The menu wireframe defined horizontal filter tabs and a horizontal item card layout (image left, details right, CTA right-aligned). The hi-fi executed this with food photography filling the thumbnail zone and an orange pill-style "Add +" button — large enough to tap without precision, never crowded by surrounding content.

C

Cart, Checkout & Confirmation

Earning the close — minimal friction, maximum reassurance

The wireframe flagged the empty cart as a full-screen state with a single CTA. Rather than leaving it as an error, the hi-fi turned it into a brand moment: a warm illustration, empathetic copy, and a single "Browse menu" button — the only CTA on screen. No confusion about what to do next.

The wireframe structured checkout as three clearly grouped sections: delivery fields, payment options, and a pay CTA. The hi-fi respected this grouping with rounded card containers separating each section. Three payment methods — card, bank transfer, and pay on delivery — were included to reflect the Nigerian market's diverse payment behaviour. No multi-step wizard, no unnecessary friction.

The confirmation wireframe placed a check badge, order details, and a primary CTA. The hi-fi added a delivery rider illustration above the order info — a moment of visual delight that turns a transactional receipt into a reassuring handoff. "Track order" is the primary action; "Back to Home" is a secondary text link that keeps forward momentum without competing.

06
Final Polish & Takeaways

What the Final
Deliverable Achieved

The completed screen set was handed off as a fully annotated, production-ready hi-fi prototype — every component built to brand spec, every interaction state considered. The client received a product that looked exactly like the app they had imagined. The development team received something they could build from without ambiguity.

More critically, the design proved that a vibrant, personality-rich UI and a frictionless user experience are not opposites. The energy of the Chuks Kitchen brand comes through in the food photography, the CTA colour, the warm illustration style, and the confident copy tone — not in visual clutter or decorative excess.

📐

A brief is a design tool

The BRD wasn't a constraint — it was a foundation. Mapping every requirement to a UI decision kept the design focused and review cycles efficient.

🎯

Colour earns its use

Orange works hardest when it marks the most important action on screen. The moment it appears everywhere, it stops meaning anything.

📝

Document every assumption

The BRD had gaps. Every design assumption was made explicit and flagged — giving the client confidence and protecting the decisions in review.

🌍

₦ pricing, Nigerian food photography, and payment options suited to the local market made the product feel real and trustworthy to its actual users.

🔲

Wireframes protect the idea

Lo-fi first meant the layout logic was bulletproof before colour or photography arrived. The hi-fi elevated structure that was already sound.

✨

Empty states are brand moments

The empty cart and verification screens could have been cold. Instead they became small moments of warmth that build trust and reinforce personality.

The lo-fi defined the logic. The colour system set the rules. The hi-fi brought it to life. Every layer of the design process was in service of one thing: getting hungry people to their food faster.
