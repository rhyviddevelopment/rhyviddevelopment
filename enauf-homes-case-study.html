Project Overview

A Property Platform
Built for Lagos

Enauf Homes is a Lagos-based real estate platform connecting buyers, renters, and short-let seekers with verified property listings across the city's most sought-after neighbourhoods — Victoria Island, Lekki, Ikoyi, Yaba, and beyond. With a growing inventory and an increasingly mobile-first audience, the platform needed a digital presence that could match its ambition.

The original website had been built on a generic template that served the business in its early days but had outgrown its usefulness. The interface felt dated, the search experience was buried, and the visual design failed to communicate the trust and professionalism that property seekers expect before committing to a viewing — let alone a purchase.

Before a single wireframe was drawn, I spent time auditing the existing website — mapping where users were likely dropping off and identifying the core trust and usability failures that were costing the business leads.

Weak Visual Hierarchy

Typography was inconsistent, CTAs were low-contrast, and the layout lacked a clear reading path — making it hard to know where to look or what to do next.

The site had not been optimised for mobile use despite the majority of Nigerian property seekers browsing on their phones first.

Core design goal: Make it effortless for a first-time visitor to understand what Enauf Homes offers, trust the platform, and take action — whether that's searching for a property, browsing listings, or exploring a neighbourhood — within the first scroll.

Before vs After — Key UX Shifts

Before
  • Generic hero banner with no clear search CTA above the fold
  • No distinction between Buy, Rent, and Short-let journeys
  • Listing cards showed minimal information — no location tags
  • Blog and listings competed for equal visual weight
  • Footer was dense, unorganised, and hard to navigate
→
After
  • Hero leads with a full-width search bar, tabbed by listing type, above the fold
  • Buy / Rent / Short-let tabs give users an instant mental model of the platform
  • Listing cards show property type, price, and area badge prominently
  • Blog is visually separated with its own section hierarchy
  • Footer structured into five logical columns with clear link categories

Design Decisions

Every Choice
Had a Reason

The redesign wasn't a visual refresh — it was a structural rethink. Each major UI decision was made in service of a specific user behaviour or business outcome.

Navy Blue Primary

Trust is the first job of a real estate brand. Navy blue communicates stability, authority, and professionalism — the exact signals a user needs to feel confident enough to submit their contact details or request a viewing. The existing brand used navy and it was carried forward deliberately, strengthened with more consistent application across all UI elements.

Orange for CTAs Only

Orange appears exclusively on primary calls-to-action — the search button, listing badges, and key action buttons. This creates a clear visual hierarchy where every interactive element that matters is immediately findable. Orange against navy is high-contrast, WCAG-compliant, and energetic without undermining the brand's professional tone.

Hero Search Above the Fold

The single most important action on a property platform is the search. Moving the search bar — complete with Buy / Rent / Short-let tabs — to the hero section above the fold means every visitor, regardless of intent, is immediately given the tools they need. The tabs also serve as a taxonomy guide: users who didn't know "short-let" was an option now discover it passively.

City Exploration Grid

Lagos property seekers often search by neighbourhood first — they know they want Lekki or Ikoyi before they know what type of property they want. The Explore Cities grid at the bottom of the page caters directly to this mental model, presenting eight key Lagos areas as visual entry points into filtered listing views. It reduces the cognitive load of the search bar for users who are still in discovery mode.

Featured Listings Layout

The listing cards use a mixed-size grid — one large hero card alongside two smaller cards — rather than a uniform three-column grid. This creates visual rhythm and gives premium or featured properties a natural stage without requiring a separate "featured" banner. The "150+ Properties" overflow card acts as a passive CTA, communicating inventory depth while directing users deeper into the platform.

"On a real estate platform, trust and speed are the only two things that matter. Every design decision was made to establish one or enable the other."

Colour & Visual System

A Palette Built
for Conversion

The colour system was designed with a single principle: every colour should either build trust or direct action — never just decorate.

Navy Primary
#0D2D6B

Navigation, hero backgrounds, section anchors. Communicates authority and trust.

Orange CTA
#F47B1F

Search buttons, listing badges, primary actions. Drives user interaction.

Off-White Base
#F8F9FC

Page background. Slightly warm — softer than pure white, easier on the eye for long browsing sessions.

Ink Dark
#1A1F2E

Body text and headings. Near-black with a blue undertone that ties to the navy brand colour.

Section Annotations — What Each UI Zone Does

Hero Section

Full-width navy + photography background with the search bar as the centrepiece. Establishes brand colour, communicates platform purpose, and captures user intent in one zone.

Why Choose Us

Three numbered value propositions on a mid-navy background. Builds trust before the user reaches listings — addresses objections before they form.

Featured Listings

Mixed-size card grid on an off-white background. The contrast with the navy section above creates a visual breath — signalling a shift from "why us" to "here's the product."

Explore Cities Grid

Eight Lagos neighbourhood tiles with real photography. Caters to location-first browsers and adds credibility through specificity — this isn't a generic property site, it knows Lagos.

Takeaways & Reflections

What This Project
Reinforced

Redesigning a live product is a different discipline from designing something new. There are expectations to manage, brand equities to preserve, and existing users whose mental models you cannot break. This project sharpened several instincts that now inform how I approach all web UI work.

🏗️

Structure before style

The most impactful changes in this redesign were structural — where the search bar lives, how listings are grouped, how the footer is organised. Visual polish came second.

🎯

One CTA colour, one job

Giving orange a single, exclusive role — primary actions only — made every button on the page feel more intentional. Restraint in colour use creates clarity.

🗺️

Design for local mental models

Lagos property seekers think in neighbourhoods first. The Explore Cities grid wasn't a decorative feature — it was a direct response to how the actual target audience searches.

📐

Mixed-size grids create rhythm

Breaking away from uniform card grids added visual interest and created a natural hierarchy within the listings section — without needing separate "featured" labels.

🤝

Trust is designed, not assumed

The "Why Choose Us" section positioned above the listings wasn't accidental. On a platform handling high-value transactions, credibility must be established before the product is shown.

📱

Mobile-first thinking changes everything

Designing with the mobile user as the primary case forced better decisions about information density, tap target sizes, and scroll pacing that improved the desktop version too.

"A good redesign doesn't feel like a redesign to the user — it just feels like a website that finally works the way it should have all along."
